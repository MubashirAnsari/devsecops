# Passbolt – Secrets Management Case Study

## Context
In 2025, I repeatedly encountered a common but risky pattern in day-to-day operations:
- Credentials shared via chat messages and documents
- “Temporary” access that quietly became permanent
- No clear ownership of shared secrets

While these practices helped teams move fast, they introduced long-term security and audit risks.

## Objective
The objective was to bring **structure and accountability** to secrets management without creating friction for engineering teams.

Key goals:
- Centralize secret storage
- Define clear ownership of credentials
- Enforce role-based access and least privilege
- Improve audit readiness and incident response

## Approach
I introduced **Passbolt** as a centralized secrets management solution, focusing as much on **process change** as on tooling.

High-level approach:
- Centralized storage for shared credentials
- Role-based access aligned with team responsibilities
- Defined ownership and lifecycle for each secret
- Reduced ad-hoc credential sharing across channels

The emphasis was on changing habits, not just adopting a new tool.

## Outcome
- Reduced credential sprawl across chats and documents
- Improved visibility into who has access to which secrets
- Clearer accountability during access reviews
- Stronger confidence during audits and security incidents

Security improved without slowing down delivery.

## Key Learnings
- Secrets management is a people problem before it is a technical one
- Clear ownership reduces both risk and confusion
- Removing risky shortcuts is more effective than enforcing rigid controls

## Control Alignment
- ISO 27001:2022 – Access Control
- Secrets Management Best Practices
- Least Privilege Principle
- Auditability & Accountability

## Notes
This case study is intentionally anonymized and generalized.
No internal credentials, system names, domains, IPs, or configurations are included.

The focus is on **security decision-making and outcomes**, not on exposing sensitive details.
